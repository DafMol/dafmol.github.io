Thomas Hobbes (1588-1679)

An English philosopher who is often seen as one of the earliest thinkers of modern political philosophy. His most famous work without a doubt is the Leviathan. In it he describes why humankind needs political states and he shows how the power-balance, rights and duties should be divided between the modern political sovereign and his(/her) commonwealth.

[On life in the state of nature, without a sovereign ruler:] "No arts; no letters; no society; and which is worst of all, continual fear and danger of violent death; the life of man solitary, poor, nasty, brutish and short." - Leviathan I XIII 9

Michel Foucault (1926-1984)

A French philosopher who is known for his history-focussed methodology (Genealogies). His works mainly target the relationships that exist between power, knowledge and social institutions in societies all throughout human history. His most famous works is Discipline and Punish, wherein he describes how societies have punished criminals or dissonants and how changes in these methods of punishment align with changes in societal power structures. He argues that modern societies have increasingly become the equivalents of prisons thanks to subtle changes in locus and structure of societal power.

[On similarities between social institutions and how they all serve to manifest power and surveillance induced conformity:] "Is it surprising that prisons resemble factories, schools, barracks and hospitals, which - in turn - all resemble prisons?" - Discipline and Punish

Theodor Adorno (1903-1969)

A German philosopher and sociologist who is often seen as one of the most prominent early post-war thinkers to propose critical societal theories. His magnus opus is Dialectic of Enlightenment, an attempted modernization and application of Marx's critiques of capitalism on contemporary late stage capitalism. He details amongst others how art and culture have changed over time to be mere tools of late state capitalist power structures; they have become capitalist commodities that rob people of their imagination and enforce conformity.

"The power of the culture industry's ideology is such that conformity has replaced consciousness. The order that springs from it is never confronted with what it claims to be or with the real interests of human beings." - Culture Industry Reconsidered
